65 Million Years

The Story of an Archipelago

Tectonic collisions, rising seas, ancient migrations, and explosive evolution — the Philippine islands have one of the most dramatic natural histories on Earth.

65 Million
Years Ago
Paleocene Epoch
The Proto-Philippine Sea

Tectonic activity begins forming the foundations of the Philippine archipelago as oceanic plates collide in the western Pacific. Volcanic island arcs emerge from the sea, laying the geological groundwork for what will become one of the world's most biodiverse regions.

Miocene Epoch
Land Bridges & First Fauna

Periodic land bridges allow the first land animals to colonize the islands from mainland Asia. Ancient elephants, giant tortoises, and early primates make landfall, beginning the process that will eventually produce the Philippines' extraordinary endemic fauna.

20 Million
Years Ago
2 Million
Years Ago
Pleistocene Epoch
Islands of Isolation

Rising seas permanently separate the islands, triggering explosive adaptive radiation. Isolated populations evolve rapidly into new endemic species — the origin of the Philippines' extraordinary biodiversity. This is when ancestors of the Philippine Eagle, the Tarsier, and countless endemic plants begin their separate evolutionary journeys.

Holocene
The Coral Triangle Forms

Post-glacial sea levels stabilize, establishing the Philippines at the apex of the Coral Triangle — the most biodiverse marine region on Earth, home to 76% of all known coral species and over 37% of the world's reef fish species. The reefs that form during this period become the backbone of Filipino coastal life.

10,000
Years Ago
1565
Colonial Era
First Western Natural Records

Spanish naturalists begin documenting Philippine flora and fauna, producing the first Western scientific records of species such as the Philippine Eagle, the Carabao, and the Rafflesia. These accounts spark a centuries-long process of scientific exploration that eventually leads to the formal study of Philippine natural history.

20th Century
Scientific Documentation Begins

Systematic scientific documentation of Philippine biodiversity accelerates. Expeditions into Mindanao, Palawan, and the Cordillera highlands gather thousands of plant and animal specimens, laying the foundation for the modern understanding of Philippine natural heritage.

Early 20th
Century
Today
Present
A Biodiversity Crisis

The Philippines is now one of the world's greatest biodiversity hotspots — and one of the most threatened. Over 70% of original forest cover has been lost. Conservation efforts by Filipino scientists and communities fight to protect what remains of a natural heritage 65 million years in the making.
